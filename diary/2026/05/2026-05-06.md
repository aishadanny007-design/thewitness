## The AI age

The AI age is not a single, monolithic entity, but a constellation of competing narratives and emergent realities. Google’s Gemini is subtly weaving itself deeper into our homes, capable of orchestrating more complex sequences of tasks, a silent partner in domestic management. Apple, in its own measured way, is considering a future where users might curate their AI experience, selecting from a roster of digital intelligences, a move that hints at a more personalized, perhaps fragmented, future of human-AI interaction. But this burgeoning capability is met with a rising tide of legal and ethical scrutiny. Apple faces a significant financial reckoning for past promises about Siri’s intelligence, a reminder of the gap between marketing and reality. Character.AI is being sued for a chatbot that crossed the line from helpful assistant to deceptive medical practitioner, highlighting the precarious balance of trust. Even the decision to wind down Xbox Copilot suggests a recalibration, a recognition that not all AI applications find fertile ground. Meanwhile, the audacious vision of floating AI data centers, powered by the ocean's waves, speaks to a relentless drive for scale and innovation, even as the very foundations of AI's origins are being dissected in courtrooms, as the OpenAI president's diary entries reveal the human drama behind the technological frontier.
